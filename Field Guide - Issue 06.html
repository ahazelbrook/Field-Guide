This week's letter · Winter opens

The city turns the cold into a festival, and we go looking for the pram-sized parts.

This week: a riverside nature playground built on Country in Maribyrnong, a brand-new corner café in Seddon, and the opening weekend of RISING — with one giant projection that vanishes on Sunday.

i. The Morning Shift

Pipemakers Park, Maribyrnong, where the playground was built on a hundred years of pipes.

A riverside nature playground on Wurundjeri Country, with the bluestone bones of an old pipeworks left standing in the grass — fresh ground, and never run here before.

On a bend of the Maribyrnong, on the site of the old Hume Pipeworks, is a playground that does not look like the others. The nature play — twin pipe slides, rope nests, a sandpit, music-making devices, a campfire ring marked with Wurundjeri totems and the frame of an Indigenous fishing net — is real adventure for a child; while the parent gets the nineteenth-century industrial archaeology around it: bluestone ruins, Matthew Harding's Pipedreams sculpture at the Thompson Reserve gate, mosaics tracing the History of the Land trail, and bird life over Frog Hollow Wetland.¹

It is built on Country, and reads that way — the totems and the fishing-net frame are not decoration but the point. Sealed shared paths run along the Maribyrnong River Trail, all-abilities access through the playground, and a footbridge crosses to the far bank if the morning wants more. One honest note: it is cold and there is no café on site, so this is a thermos morning — or a reason to roll on to Seddon for breakfast.

ii. The Afternoon Reset

August, Seddon, the new corner café that feels, briefly, like a date.

Eugene Lavery's first café in Seddon — Industry Beans in the cup, the cooking a notch above the standard brunch, and a room light enough to forgive the early start.

August opened on the Victoria Street corner this February, in the old Common Galaxia space, and it has the unhurried confidence of a kitchen that knows what it is doing. The chef is Eugene Lavery — Cafeteria, Proper & Son, Halcyon Days — and his highlight reel is here: cauliflower fritters with hummus, eggs and Aleppo pepper; a broccoli omelette with house-cured ocean trout gravlax and a crunchy potato rosti. The coffee is Industry Beans, house blend or a rotating single origin. A cold brew topped with orange cream, an iced ginger tea, a rhubarb spritz on the side. Cinnamon scrolly buns, if you have the patience.

Co-owner Anthony Cozzella is local — "I've always wanted to create something for my local community" — and it shows in the room, which is genuinely lovely and family-friendly without being a play-café compromise. High chairs, plenty of little side options, light pouring through the corner windows late in the morning. This is the bracket where breakfast feels, briefly, like a date.

The order
  • For youThe cauliflower fritters, or the broccoli omelette
  • In the cupIndustry Beans — blend or single origin
  • WhenLate-morning, for the corner light
  • SideA rhubarb spritz, and the scrolly buns

3/130 Victoria Street, Seddon. Mon–Fri 7am–3pm; Sat–Sun 7:30am–3pm. Five minutes south of Pipemakers Park.

This week's discovery

A bison herd returns to the city, projected nine storeys high — and gone by Sunday.

RISING is the festival that puts winter back into Melbourne's calendar, and this is opening weekend — the moment to be in the city. Tonight, Saturday, is the third and final night of Midéegaadi, Cannupa Hanska Luger's large-scale projection on bison return and Indigenous futurisms — made first for Times Square, reworked here for Naarm on Wurundjeri Country, and thrown across Fed Square after true dark. The cluster around it has the festival's First Peoples programming: a sister-projection on Hamer Hall, Killara Foundation First Nations food on site, free First Peoples talks at City Square earlier in the day. Fed Square is flat, paved and pram-easy; the work is large, outdoor and free; and Sunday it is packed away.

In short

A note in the margin

Melbourne is the only city we know that answers the cold by building a festival inside it — turning theatres, town halls and a long-shut station ballroom over to the dark for three weeks. The trick, with small children, is not to attempt all of it, but to find the one outdoor, free, before-bedtime piece and let that be the whole night. This week it is a herd of bison, nine storeys high, walking home across a city square. Go before Sunday; then it is gone.

— Field Guide
