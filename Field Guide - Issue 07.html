This week's letter · The long weekend

A long weekend is permission to go the long way round.

Three spare days are the rare kind of time that lets you finally drive west to the hangar you keep meaning to visit, or take a boat across the bay to a fire on a foreshore. This week: the airfield where Australia learned to fly, a far-west kitchen worth the detour, a Pasifika block party closing the city's winter festival, and a Celtic weekend you reach by ferry.

i. The Morning Shift

The airfield where Australia learned to fly, with the aircraft still in it.

An hour in the hangars at Point Cook — and on a Sunday, the rare sight of a heritage aircraft actually taking off.

Point Cook is where the Royal Australian Air Force began, on the country's first military airfield, and its museum keeps more than a century of aircraft in two great hangars¹ — First World War biplanes, Vietnam-era jets, a wall of ejector seats and survival kits, trench displays that bring the history uncomfortably close. Entry is free and you can get right up to most of it, which is the part that holds a four-year-old and a thirty-five-year-old equally: the planes are enormous, the stories underneath them are real, and the whole thing is indoors and out of the June cold.

The reason to come on a Sunday is the one o'clock flying display, when a heritage aircraft is wheeled out and flown — engine start, the smell of it, a few minutes in the air, a pilot taking questions afterwards. Two honest notes. It sits on an active air base, so you book ahead and bring photo identification; and there is no café on site, only an undercover picnic table, so pack a thermos and something for them to eat.

While you're there

  • b.Point Cook HomesteadOne of the bay's oldest farmhouses, a few minutes back up the road.

ii. The Afternoon Reset

An Albion corner that feeds you like a friend's mother would.

A small far-west room worth the detour — Allpress in the cup, a burger regulars won't stop talking about, and colouring pages for them.

Sadie Black is run by Albion locals Chris and Meaghan Blackwell — named, as these places often are, after the dog — and the menu is short, seasonal and done properly rather than cleverly. The SB Burger is the one people press on you, the Ruben sells out before lunch, and the corn fritters come with a tomato chutney regulars ask for extra of. There is a courtyard for when the sun's out, a cabinet for when it isn't, and colouring pages on the table, which on a long weekend counts for a great deal.

The order
  • For youThe SB Burger, or the corn fritters with poached eggs, avocado, crumbed feta & that tomato chutney
  • For themAn egg-and-bacon roll, or a babyccino while the kitchen works
  • WhenLate morning, in the courtyard if the weather allows
  • SideAllpress coffee; tea from Apollo Bay's Tea and Scandal

Ask after the Ruben early; by late morning it has usually gone. Albion is on the way back from Point Cook.

iii. · This week's discovery

A Celtic weekend across the bay, best reached by boat.

The National Celtic Festival takes over Portarlington for the whole long weekend, and the part that works with children is free: from ten in the morning the foreshore fills with food trucks, open fires, free live music, markets, highland cattle and a proper children's program, while the ticketed concerts and workshops — everything from limerick-writing to learning the bagpipes — carry the grown-up half of it. The good trick is how you get there: Port Phillip Ferries run from Docklands straight to Portarlington, and the crossing turns the journey into the day out — the boat will outrank the bagpipes in the back-seat retelling. It is winter on an exposed foreshore, so the fires earn their keep and a coat is not optional; book the ferry before you count on it.

iv. Or, this week's other discovery

Or stay in town, where the city throws one last party.

RISING signs off with a free, all-ages Pasifika takeover of Fed Square — and they will teach you the steps.

God Save the Queens closes the city's winter festival, and it is the rare big event built to be joined rather than watched. The Royal Family Dance Crew — Parris Goebel's crew, the ones behind the Sorry video and two Super Bowl half-times — perform, then walk the whole square through a routine, step by step, with a line-up of Pasifika artists keeping it going from sunset on.

One honest thing, and the reason to plan it: it begins around dusk and tips into a proper late party as the night goes on, so the family window is the early evening — the dance lesson and the first sets — and then home before it turns. Free, all ages, and Fed Square is flat enough for a pram.

A note in the margin

A long weekend is the rare sort of time that lets you go the long way round — west to a hangar, or across the water to a fire on a foreshore — without anyone watching the clock. We started Field Guide for the weekends that manage to hold both of you at once, the children and the people you were before them. Three days just give that a little more room to breathe.
