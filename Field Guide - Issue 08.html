This week's letter · Deep winter

Deep winter, and a weekend that rewards staying in.

This week: a warm neighbourhood library for a slow Saturday start, a bagel four doors along Barkly Street, a literary festival by the bay that lets the children in free — and, should words fail, a city market piled with hot donuts.

i. The Morning Shift

Start the cold one indoors, where the stories are kept.

There is a strong case, in the middle of June, for a Saturday morning that begins somewhere heated — and a library makes it better than most.

The West Footscray branch on Barkly Street is the warm-room morning we've been waiting for the cold to justify: light, quiet, recently given longer Saturday hours, with a children's collection deep enough to lose half an hour in and a Saturday storytime to start things off. No agenda beyond a coffee that stays hot and a child who has, briefly, stopped asking what's next. It is a Saturday pick — the doors stay shut on Sunday — but that is the only catch, and there's a bagel a few doors along when you're ready to leave.

ii. The Afternoon Reset

A bagel on Barkly Street, four doors from the books.

Migrant Coffee does the kind of bagel that earns the short walk in the cold.

A bagel-and-coffee shop on the same strip as the library, run with more warmth than the weather deserves. The order is the Come On — a bagel with fresh sliced ham, the sort of thing that makes you glad you skipped breakfast. The coffee's good, the room is unfussy, and nobody minds the pram in the corner.

The order
  • For youThe Come On — a bagel with fresh sliced ham
  • For themA plain bagel, toasted; a babyccino if it's that kind of morning
  • WhenMid-morning, on the way out of the library
  • SideA flat white you don't have to apologise for

The walk between the two is roughly the length of one negotiation about whether we're getting a babyccino.

iii. · This week's discovery

A festival of words by the bay — and the children get the run of it.

For one weekend the Willy Lit Fest turns the Town Hall over to writers and readers, and this year the children's program is both free and genuinely good.

The festival (est. 2003, this year themed Reflections) fills the heritage Town Hall on Ferguson Street and the rooms around it with talks, panels and workshops across Saturday and Sunday. For you, the program runs deep — Tony Birch on a new collection, Evelyn Araluen, Maxine Beneba Clarke on writing the inner west, a panel grimly titled Eulogy for the Internet that will land for anyone who used to be online for fun.

For the children, Saturday gathers a free run of sessions in the Williamstown Library Gallery: Stories Across the Flyway, multilingual tales told for all ages; Papertales: Stories on Wheels, storytelling powered, somehow, by a bicycle; and the pick of them, Create Your Own Character with Leigh Hobbs — an hour of inventing something wacky with the man who drew Mr Chicken, for the seven-to-twelves. Every children's session is free, and under-twelves get in free throughout.¹ Book ahead — sessions fill, and the festival's own program page has been slow to refresh this year, so go through the official link.

iv. This week's discovery

Or, if words fail: a market full of hot donuts.

Queen Vic Market gives over two sheds to deep-fried everything, free to wander, both days.

This is the city counterweight to the bookish bay: the C–D sheds at the Peel Street end, packed with the makers — hot cinnamon classics, mochi, loukoumades, churro bowls, a few savoury experiments for the contrarians — plus roving performers and circus to keep the sugar moving. It is busy and it is sweet, and entirely free until you start buying donuts, which you will. Go early; bring wipes.

For the grown-ups

Once the children are down, the festival keeps a night for you.

Shaun Micallef: De'Ath Takes a Holiday — Saturday 13 June, 7.00 pm, at the Town Hall — has the comedian discussing his new gothic satire, in which an immortal hopscotches through history wondering whether immortality was worth it, in conversation with Marieke Hardy. A rare night out, ten minutes from the festival you spent the day at. Tickets through the festival.

A note in the margin

There is a particular pleasure in handing a child a book and watching them vanish into it for twenty minutes — long enough, sometimes, to finish a coffee while it's still warm. This week is full of that small trade: rooms kept heated, stories read aloud, a whole festival built on the idea that words are worth gathering for. We started Field Guide to find the things that are for them and for us at once, and a library on a cold morning might be the purest version we've found yet.

— Field Guide
